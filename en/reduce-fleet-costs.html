Main fleet cost components

Four major cost groups

The amount shown on the monthly invoice is only part of the picture. In a business context, it is useful to distinguish at least acquisition or lease payment (purchase, operating lease, or financial lease), energy (fuel or electricity), maintenance and wear (servicing, tyres, repairs), and cross-cutting costs: insurance, charges associated with business use, relevant equipment, and downtime (opportunity cost). There are also indirect costs — administrative fleet management, claims, alignment with internal tax policy, and how vehicles fit within company mobility rules.

Why TCO should guide decisions

Total cost of ownership brings these categories together over a defined period — for example three or four years — and allows like-for-like comparisons: vehicle A vs. B, combustion vs. plug-in hybrid, purchase vs. leasing. Without TCO, companies often compare list price and overlook the impact of mileage profile or maintenance, which distorts decisions in fleet analysis and budgeting.

How to reduce fuel consumption

Powertrain aligned with real usage

Consumption is one of the categories most sensitive to daily context: road type, traffic, load, and driving style all measurably affect litres per hundred kilometres. First, align powertrain type with actual usage: mainly urban routes require different solutions from long motorway journeys. Second, clear internal rules — appropriate speed limits, cruise control use where applicable, and eco-driving training for higher-mileage drivers — tend to deliver fast returns at controlled cost compared with premature fleet renewal.

Consumption and refuelling management

From a management standpoint, tracking average consumption by vehicle and by driver highlights deviations that justify internal review or technical inspection (tyre pressure, mechanical condition). With conventional fuels or hybrids, refuelling through contracted networks and disciplined record-keeping reduce leakage and simplify financial reconciliation. With electric or plug-in vehicles, cost per kWh and charging location become additional factors: tariff periods or corporate agreements with charging operators may weigh more on total spend than the isolated vehicle model choice.

Maintenance optimization

Plans, schedules, and service networks

Delaying servicing or choosing workshops without criteria may look like immediate savings and often turns into higher cost: more breakdowns, higher consumption, and additional downtime. A per-vehicle plan — with schedule and mileage aligned with manufacturer guidance and with the fleet's effective operating pace — reduces unplanned immobilizations and supports negotiation of packages or terms with preferred service networks.

Fleet segmentation

Tyres and braking influence safety and consumption; tyre rotation and correct pressure are simple and effective measures. In mixed fleets (passenger and light commercial vehicles), policy segmentation helps: high-usage vehicles may justify maintenance included in leasing; low-mileage vehicles may benefit from mileage-based service intervals rather than time-based intervals alone. Keeping an intervention history helps anticipate expensive replacements and supports renewal decisions based on real historical cost.

Benefits of fleet analysis

Documented and comparable decisions

Professional fleet analysis combines operational data with company objectives: budget, brand image, mobility ESG commitments, and tax constraints. The output is not just an apparently cheaper list: it is a framework where each recommendation is grounded in total cost, usage profile, and tax risk — in other words, decision lines that withstand internal scrutiny.

Stronger negotiation position

Concrete benefits include prioritizing renewals with higher return, rationalizing fleet segments and equipment (fewer variants to manage), and preparing negotiations with suppliers or financial institutions using consolidated figures. How to get started today

Minimum data to gather

A practical first step is to gather information per vehicle or homogeneous group: average annual mileage, route type, current acquisition model, and, where available, energy and maintenance records. It is useful to define a time horizon for comparing scenarios — for example four years — and decide in advance which indicators to compare (cost per kilometre, annual cost, or total cost over period).
