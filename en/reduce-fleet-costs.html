Main fleet cost drivers

Four major expense groups

The figure on the monthly invoice is only part of the picture. In a business context, distinguish at least acquisition or rental (purchase, contract hire or finance lease), energy (fuel or electricity), maintenance and wear (servicing, tyres, repairs), and cross-cutting costs: insurance, charges linked to professional use, relevant equipment, and downtime (opportunity cost). Indirect costs add on — fleet administration, claims, alignment with internal tax policy, and how vehicles fit corporate mobility rules.

Why TCO should drive the decision

Total cost of ownership brings these items together over a defined period — say three or four years — and lets you compare scenarios on equal terms: vehicle A vs B, combustion vs plug-in hybrid, purchase vs contract hire. Without TCO, list price is often compared while mileage profile or maintenance impact is ignored, which skews decisions in fleet analysis and budgeting.

How to reduce fuel consumption

Powertrain aligned to real use

Consumption is one of the most context-sensitive lines: road type, traffic, load, and driving style materially change litres per 100 km. First, align motorization with actual use: mostly urban routes call for different solutions from long motorway runs. Second, clear internal rules — appropriate speed limits, cruise control where suitable, eco-driving training for high-mileage drivers — often pay back quickly and cheaply compared with premature fleet renewal.

Consumption and refuelling management

From a management perspective, tracking average consumption by vehicle and by driver highlights variances that warrant internal follow-up or a technical check (tyre pressure, mechanical condition). With conventional or hybrid fuels, contracted networks and disciplined logging reduce leakage and simplify finance reconciliation. For electric or plug-in vehicles, add cost per kWh and charging location: off-peak tariffs or operator agreements can weigh more on total cost than the model choice alone.

Maintenance optimization

Plans, schedules, and networks

Deferring service or picking workshops without criteria may look like short-term savings and often becomes higher cost: extra breakdowns, higher consumption, and lost days. A plan per vehicle — calendar and mileage aligned with the manufacturer and with the fleet’s real rhythm — cuts unplanned downtime and supports package deals with a preferred network.

Fleet segmentation

Tyres and braking affect safety and consumption; rotation and correct pressure are simple, effective measures. In mixed fleets (cars and vans), segment policies help: high-utilization vehicles may justify maintenance included in contract hire; low-mileage units may suit interval-based servicing rather than time alone. Keeping an intervention history anticipates expensive replacements and supports renewals grounded in real historic cost.

Benefits of fleet analysis

Documented, comparable decisions

Professional fleet analysis blends operational data with company goals: budget, brand image, mobility ESG commitments, and tax constraints. The output is not merely a cheaper-looking list: it is a framework where each recommendation rests on total cost, usage profile, and fiscal risk — decision lines that stand up to internal scrutiny.

Stronger negotiating position

Concrete benefits include prioritizing renewals with the highest return, rationalizing segments and specifications (fewer variants to manage), and preparing negotiations with suppliers or financiers using consolidated numbers. For teams under budget pressure, analysis also reduces noise: it replaces impressions with a shared baseline — TCO and the assumptions behind it — as outlined in the services presentation on the homepage.

How to start today

Minimum data to gather

A first step is to collect information per vehicle or per homogeneous group: average annual kilometres, route types, current acquisition model, and where available fuel and maintenance records. Define a time horizon to compare scenarios — e.g. four years — and upfront which indicators to compare (cost per kilometre, annual cost, or total cost over the period).
